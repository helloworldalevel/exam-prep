Question 1: Local Government Agency & Networks

Total Marks: 14 | Exam Time: ~18 minutes

📘 Context

A local government agency uses IT in its operations. Employees work from the office, from home, or on the move. The agency manages employee collaboration, data storage, and network connectivity across multiple locations.

1(a)(i): MCQ — Network Storage 1m

Employees store and access files using a local area network (LAN). Identify the type of storage used. A HDMI · B NAS · C RAID · D SSID

Context

Employees need shared storage accessible across the LAN. You must identify which of four acronyms represents "type of storage accessed over a LAN".

Key Terms & Concepts

NAS (Network-Attached Storage) — A dedicated file-storage device plugged into the LAN, accessible by all users on the network.
HDMI — High-Definition Multimedia Interface — a video/audio cable/port, nothing to do with storage.
RAID — Redundant Array of Inexpensive Disks — multiple physical disks combined into one logical disk for redundancy/speed. A disk layout, not network storage.
SSID — Service Set Identifier — the human-readable name of a Wi-Fi network (e.g. "HomeRouter-5G"). Not storage.

✓ Mark Scheme Answer

B (NAS)

Mark Breakdown: NAS (1 mark)

💡 Hints & Exam Tips

  • For MCQs, cross out obviously wrong answers first — here, HDMI and SSID have nothing to do with storage.
  • Key distinction: NAS = network-accessible, RAID = multiple disks as one. The question says "using a LAN" which points to NAS.
  • Remember: NAS is accessed over a network; RAID is configured as a storage system.

⚠️ Common Mistakes

  • Do not confuse NAS with RAID — A NAS device often uses RAID internally, but they are fundamentally different. The question asks about the type of storage accessed over the LAN, which is NAS.
  • Do not pick RAID just because it mentions "disks" — RAID is a storage configuration, not a network storage type.
1(a)(ii): Two Collaboration Methods 2m

State two other ways employees can work together to collaborate in an online environment (other than file storage in the cloud).

Context

You already know they store files in the cloud — name two different collaboration methods besides that. Each method is worth 1 mark.

Key Terms & Concepts

Collaboration tools — Software or services that let multiple people contribute to the same work, either at the same time (synchronously) or one after another (asynchronously).
Video conferencing — Live video meetings (e.g. Zoom, Teams, Google Meet).
VOIP — Voice Over Internet Protocol — making phone calls using the internet rather than the traditional telephone network.
Instant messaging / chat — Real-time text exchange apps (e.g. Slack, Teams chat, WhatsApp).

✓ Mark Scheme Answer

Any two of these (1 mark each):

  • Work on the same document collaboratively / at the same time
  • Use video conferencing to hold meetings
  • Use VOIP to make (free) telephone calls
  • Use email / shared storage to exchange or share files or data or resources
  • Use instant messaging / chat applications to exchange messages

Mark Breakdown: Any two from the list above (1 mark each)

💡 Hints & Exam Tips

  • Give two distinctly different methods, not two instances of the same idea. For example, "Zoom and Teams" is really one method (video conferencing), not two.
  • Naming a specific app (e.g. "Google Docs") is fine — examiners map it to its category (shared document editing).
  • Use clear language: "Video conferencing" and "Instant messaging" are both acceptable as distinct methods.

⚠️ Common Mistakes

  • Do not accept professional online communities — The question specifies collaboration tools, not community forums.
  • Do not write the storage method again (cloud file storage) — The stem says "other ways".
  • Do not write only one method — you must give two distinct methods to get 2 marks.
1(b): Positive & Negative Impact 2m

Give one positive and one negative impact of employees using online communities during work time.

Context

Two answers, one per line. Positive goes on the "Positive" line, negative on the "Negative" line. 1 mark each.

Key Terms & Concepts

Online professional community — A forum/platform where professionals share knowledge and advice in their industry (e.g. Stack Overflow, LinkedIn groups, specialist Slack channels).

✓ Mark Scheme Answer

POSITIVE (any one):

  • Employees could get help with problems from other community members
  • Employees could find new or better resources to use in their own work
  • Employees could keep up to date on new products, legislation or news
  • Employees positively promote the image of the agency, industry or profession

NEGATIVE (any one):

  • Employees waste time / are unproductive / are distracted
  • Employees may find other employment opportunities
  • Employees could give away or reveal company-sensitive information

Mark Breakdown: Positive (1 mark) + Negative (1 mark)

💡 Hints & Exam Tips

  • Write each point clearly on the correct line. Positive on "Positive", negative on "Negative".
  • One sentence each is enough — no need for long explanations.
  • Use active language: "could get", "could find", "could help".

⚠️ Common Mistakes

  • Do not award both marks for opposites. E.g. "Positive: share knowledge / Negative: share confidential info" is really the same point flipped — examiners give you 1 mark maximum.
  • If you put a negative on the positive line (or two positives), you get 1 mark maximum — read the line label before writing.
  • Do not write vague answers like "it's good" or "it's bad" — be specific about what impact.
1(c)(i): Data Concerns (Linked Explanation) 2m

Explain one reason people may be concerned that the agency collects and stores data about them.

Context

This is a classic 2-mark "linked explanation" question. You need a reason (effect) AND a because-clause (reason) that supports it. Both halves must link together logically. The question is worth 2 marks because you need to show two distinct ideas joined together.

Key Terms & Concepts

Linked explanation — A two-part answer where both halves are connected. Use "because" or "so that" to join them. The second mark is only awarded if the expansion associates with the statement.
Data protection — Laws (like GDPR/UK Data Protection Act) that govern how organisations must collect, store, and use personal data.
Personal profiling — Building a detailed picture of an individual from multiple data points, used for automated decision-making or targeted advertising.

Step-by-Step Walkthrough

  1. Pick one specific concern — don't try to cover everything.
  2. State the concern (effect) — what could go wrong.
  3. Write "because" and give the reason — why it could go wrong.
  4. Re-read to check both halves actually connect.

✓ Mark Scheme Answer

Award up to two marks for a LINKED explanation such as:

  • The data may be vulnerable to malicious behaviours / unauthorised access / hacking (1) because it is not stored securely (1)
  • The data could be shared with / sold to third parties (1) because the agency does not adhere to data-protection laws (1)
  • The data could be used to automate decision making (1) because the agency can create personal profiles (1)

Mark Breakdown: Statement (1 mark) + linked reason (1 mark). Both must associate.

💡 Hints & Exam Tips

  • Structure your answer as: [effect] because [reason].
  • If you run out of time, writing at least one half earns 1 mark — don't leave it blank.
  • Use exam vocabulary: "vulnerable to", "hacked", "data-protection laws", "third parties".
  • Make your link explicit — use connective words: "because", "so that", "which means".

⚠️ Common Mistakes

  • Do not accept "consent". Saying "because people didn't consent" is explicitly not awarded by the mark scheme.
  • Circular reasoning costs marks — e.g. "companies misuse data because they might misuse it" is saying the same thing twice.
  • Only one half of the linked explanation (e.g. only the statement, without the reason) earns 1 mark. You need both to earn 2.
1(c)(ii): Inclusion in IT Systems 2m

Describe what is meant by the term "inclusion" in the context of information technology systems.

Context

This is a "describe" question, so you need detail — a one-word definition won't get 2 marks. You need to show that you understand who needs inclusive access and why it matters.

Key Terms & Concepts

Inclusion — Designing and providing IT systems so that people from all backgrounds, ages, abilities, and circumstances can access and use them equally.
Accessibility — Specific features (screen readers, magnifiers, captions, alternative inputs) that remove barriers for users with disabilities.
Digital divide — The gap between people who have access to modern IT and those who do not (economic, geographic, age-related).

✓ Mark Scheme Answer

Award up to two marks for a linked description such as:

  • Providing everyone with equal access to devices / data / resources (1) regardless of personal circumstances — economic / race / age / gender / disability / religion / sexual orientation / cultural beliefs (1)
  • Providing special facilities for accessing IT to people (1) with personal circumstances — economic / race / age / gender / disability / religion / sexual orientation / cultural beliefs (1)

Mark Breakdown: General statement about access (1) + reference to the kinds of circumstances (1)

💡 Hints & Exam Tips

  • Name at least one specific type of circumstance (disability, age, economic background) to show you understand what inclusion covers.
  • Think of real examples: screen readers for blind users, subtitles for deaf users, large text for elderly users, free Wi-Fi for low-income users.
  • Use the word "regardless" or "regardless of" to show your understanding of universal access.

⚠️ Common Mistakes

  • Just writing "inclusion means everyone can use it" is too vague — you need the "regardless of X" or "special facilities for Y" detail to earn the second mark.
  • Do not confuse inclusion with mere usability — inclusion is about reaching everyone, not just making it easy to use.
1(d)(i): Network Metrics (Table) 4m

Complete the table to identify the network metric described in the first column. Four cells, 1 mark each.

Context

Four short-answer cells, 1 mark each. You must memorise these four network metrics because they come up year after year.

Key Terms & Concepts

Error rate — A measure of the number of bits transferred incorrectly (or proportion of bits received in error). Measured in BER (Bit Error Rate) or as a percentage.
Latency — The time delay between a data packet leaving the source and arriving at the destination. Measured in milliseconds (ms). Low latency = fast response.
Bandwidth — The maximum capacity of a communications channel — how much data it can carry per second. Measured in bits per second (bps, Mbps, Gbps).
Jitter — The variation in time between packets arriving at the destination. High jitter causes choppy video/audio calls even if bandwidth is fine.

✓ Mark Scheme Answer

  • Row 1 — "number of bits transferred incorrectly": Error rate (1 mark)
  • Row 2 — "time delay between packet leaving source and arriving at destination": Latency (1 mark)
  • Row 3 — "capacity of a communications channel": Bandwidth (1 mark)
  • Row 4 — "variation in times between packets arriving": Jitter (1 mark)

Mark Breakdown: 1 mark for each correct cell

💡 Hints & Exam Tips

  • Mnemonic: "Error, Latency, Bandwidth, Jitter" — in that order matches rows about: correctness, delay, capacity, variation.
  • Bandwidth = capacity (maximum). Latency = time to travel (delay). These two are often confused.
  • Think of it this way: Bandwidth is like the width of a pipe (capacity), Latency is like the length of the pipe (travel time).

⚠️ Common Mistakes

  • Don't write "speed" for bandwidth — bandwidth is capacity, not speed. (A 1 Gbps connection with 500ms latency is "fast" in capacity but "slow" in response.)
  • Don't write "throughput" for bandwidth — throughput is actual data transferred, bandwidth is the maximum possible.
  • Don't confuse Latency and Jitter — Latency is the base delay, Jitter is the variation in that delay.
1(d)(ii): WWW Feature (MCQ) 1m

Identify which one of these is a feature of the world wide web. A E-mail · B Routers · C TCP/IP · D URLs

Context

A classic "Internet vs WWW" question. The World Wide Web is the hypertext system (websites, links, browsers) that runs on top of the Internet. The Internet is the underlying network.

Key Terms & Concepts

Internet — The global network of networks — the underlying infrastructure (cables, routers, protocols).
World Wide Web (WWW) — A system of interlinked documents accessed via browsers using URLs, HTML, and HTTP. Built on top of the Internet.
URL — Uniform Resource Locator — the web address (e.g. https://example.com/page) that identifies a resource on the WWW.
TCP/IP — Transmission Control Protocol / Internet Protocol — the core protocol suite of the Internet (not specifically the WWW).

✓ Mark Scheme Answer

D (URLs)

Mark Breakdown: URLs (1 mark)

💡 Hints & Exam Tips

  • Memorise the distinction: email runs on SMTP/IMAP (Internet), routers are hardware (Internet infrastructure), TCP/IP is a transport protocol (Internet), URLs are specifically a WWW concept.
  • If it needs a browser, it's probably WWW. If it doesn't, it's probably just "Internet".
  • URL uniquely identifies resources on the WWW — that's the key feature of the WWW that distinguishes it from other Internet services.

⚠️ Common Mistakes

  • Common trap: Students pick "E-mail" because they associate it with the web. Email predates the WWW and uses its own protocols — it is an Internet service, not a WWW feature.
  • TCP/IP is the most tempting wrong answer if you don't know the distinction — it's a protocol, not a feature of the WWW specifically.
  • Routers are hardware infrastructure for the Internet, not a feature of the WWW.

Question 2: Networks, Encryption & Connectivity

Total Marks: 16 | Exam Time: ~21 minutes | Note: 2(c) is a major diagram worth 10 marks

📘 Context

Organisations connect devices to networks. Networks are protected by a variety of security measures. This question covers encryption techniques, anti-malware detection methods, network protocols, and connectivity scenarios.

2(a): Encryption Techniques (Table) 2m

Complete the table to identify the encryption technique: (row 1) sender and receiver use a shared key to encrypt and decrypt a message; (row 2) sender uses a public key to encrypt, receiver uses a private key to decrypt.

Context

Two rows, 1 mark each. The two techniques are symmetric and asymmetric — the question is which one goes in which row.

Key Terms & Concepts

Symmetric encryption — Both sender and receiver use the same secret key to encrypt and decrypt. Fast but the key must be shared securely beforehand.
Asymmetric encryption — Uses a pair of keys — a public key (shared with anyone, used to encrypt) and a private key (kept secret, used to decrypt). Slower but solves the key-distribution problem.

✓ Mark Scheme Answer

  • Row 1 (shared key): Symmetric (1 mark)
  • Row 2 (public key to encrypt, private key to decrypt): Asymmetric (1 mark)

Mark Breakdown: 1 mark for each correct cell

💡 Hints & Exam Tips

  • Memory trick: Symmetric = Same key. Both start with S.
  • Asymmetric = Across two keys. A for "two-sided".
  • Accept "symmetrical" and "asymmetrical" — examiners allow the -al ending.

⚠️ Common Mistakes

  • Do not accept "certificate-based" in either cell.
  • Do not accept "private" or "shared" as synonyms for symmetric.
  • Do not accept "public" alone for asymmetric.
  • Classic mistake: Swapping the two. Always double-check by re-reading the row descriptions against your answers.
2(b): Anti-Malware Detection (Linked) 2m

Some anti-malware software uses a database of known malware signatures to identify malicious software. Describe one other way anti-malware software could identify malicious software.

Context

The question rules out signature-based detection. You need to describe another detection method — and because it is worth 2 marks, it must be a linked description (method + how it works).

Key Terms & Concepts

Signature-based detection — Compares files to a database of known malware "fingerprints". Limitation: cannot catch brand-new malware.
Heuristic analysis — Examines the structure or behaviour of a program against a set of rules/characteristics to predict if it is malicious — works on unknown malware.
Behavioural monitoring — Watches what a program does while it runs, flagging suspicious activity (e.g. encrypting lots of files, modifying system settings).

✓ Mark Scheme Answer

Award up to two marks for a linked description such as:

  • Heuristics / set of rules / set of characteristics / descriptions of behaviours (1) are applied to executing software / applications (1)
  • Executing programs / applications are monitored (1) to identify unusual or harmful behaviours (1)
  • Heuristics / set of rules are used (1) to identify malicious behaviours (1)

Mark Breakdown: Detection method (1) + what it looks for / applies to (1)

💡 Hints & Exam Tips

  • Accept "running", "processing", "monitoring" as synonyms for "executing".
  • Give examples of malicious activity (e.g. "encrypting files without permission") — these count as equivalent to "behaviours".
  • Structure: [method] is used [what it does/looks for].

⚠️ Common Mistakes

  • Do not accept "sandbox" as a detection method — sandboxing is isolation, not detection.
  • Don't just write the name of a technique ("heuristics") — you need to say what it does to earn the second mark.
2(c): Network Protocols Diagram 10m

Complete the diagram by adding: a single line between devices that communicate with each other, and a label on the line stating the network communication protocol used. (Up to 10 marks.)

📘 Context — The Biggest Question on the Paper

The biggest and most valuable single question. You have to draw lines between the devices shown and label each line with the correct protocol. Each correct labelled line = 1 mark, up to 10. Thirteen possible connections are listed in the mark scheme — pick the best 10.

Scenario: Three rooms in a building — server room (wired), break room (no Wi-Fi), meeting room (Wi-Fi available). Various devices: servers, printers, phones, laptops, smart home devices, projector, remote controls.

Key Terms & Concepts — Network Protocols

Ethernet (802.3) — Wired LAN protocol — cables between servers, switches, routers, WAPs, printers.
Wi-Fi (802.11) — Wireless LAN protocol — connects laptops, phones, smart hubs to a wireless access point.
Bluetooth — Short-range wireless for peripherals (mouse, headphones, speakers). Also used for PAN tethering.
ZigBee — Low-power wireless for smart-home devices (bulbs, locks, thermostats). Often goes through a smart hub.
Cellular (GSM / 3G / 4G / 5G) — Mobile phone network — between a phone and a phone mast.
Infrared (IrDA) — Line-of-sight wireless for TV remotes, projector remotes, window-blind remotes.
USB — Wired peripheral connection (printers, keyboards, some mice).
HDMI — Wired video/audio connection (laptop to projector, laptop to monitor).

Step-by-Step Walkthrough for 2(c)

  1. Read the scenario carefully: Wireless is available in the meeting room but NOT in the break room. The printer does not support wireless.
  2. Server Room: Servers ↔ WAP with Ethernet.
  3. Break Room: Phone ↔ Phone mast with Cellular (5G/4G). Smart bulb ↔ Smart hub with ZigBee. Note: phone cannot use Wi-Fi here because the room has no wireless.
  4. Meeting Room: Laptop ↔ WAP with Wi-Fi. Laptop ↔ Mouse with Bluetooth. Laptop ↔ Headphones with Bluetooth. Printer ↔ Laptop (or Printer ↔ server) with USB (or Ethernet) — printer has no wireless.
  5. Projector: Laptop ↔ Projector with HDMI. Projector ↔ WAP with Wi-Fi (optional smart projector).
  6. Remote Controls: Remote ↔ Projector with Infrared. Remote ↔ Window blinds with Infrared.
  7. Smart Hub: Smart hub ↔ Smart lock with ZigBee. Smart hub ↔ WAP with Wi-Fi (so it can reach the internet).
  8. Count your connections — aim for 10 to maximise marks.

✓ Mark Scheme Answer — All 13 Accepted Connections

Pick the best 10:

  1. Servers ↔ WAP with Ethernet / 802.3 (1)
  2. Printer ↔ server room with Ethernet, OR Printer ↔ Laptop with USB (1)
  3. Smart lock ↔ Smart hub with ZigBee (1)
  4. Smart bulb ↔ Smart hub with ZigBee (1)
  5. Phone ↔ Phone mast with Cellular / GSM / 3G/4G/5G (1)
  6. Remote control ↔ Projector with Infrared / IrDA (1)
  7. Remote control ↔ Window blinds with Infrared / IrDA (1)
  8. Laptop ↔ Mouse with Bluetooth or USB (1)
  9. Laptop ↔ Headphones with Bluetooth or USB (1)
  10. Laptop ↔ WAP with Wi-Fi / 802.11 (1)
  11. Smart hub ↔ WAP with Wi-Fi / 802.11 (1)
  12. Laptop ↔ Projector with HDMI (1)
  13. Projector ↔ WAP with Wi-Fi / 802.11 (1)

Mark Breakdown: 1 mark per correct labelled connection, maximum 10 marks

💡 Hints & Exam Tips

  • 🚨 CRITICAL: Every line MUST have a written protocol label. An unlabelled line = 0 marks, even if the connection is correct.
  • If you find it hard to draw neatly, use a table format: From | To | Protocol. Three columns. This forces you to write a protocol for every row.
  • Allowed abbreviations: 802.11 = Wi-Fi, 802.3 = Ethernet, GSM/3G/4G/5G = Cellular, IrDA = Infrared. Mark scheme accepts both names.
  • Spelling: "Wi-Fi", "WiFi", "wifi", "Wifi" all accepted — examiners allow spelling and capitalisation flexibility.
  • Only 10 marks maximum — so 10 correct lines is a full score. Don't waste time on the 13th.

⚠️ Common Mistakes

  • Do not award marks for more than one line per pair of devices. Connecting Smart hub ↔ Smart bulb twice (once on the diagram, once in a table) doesn't give 2 marks — it gives 1.
  • Do not accept Wi-Fi from the phone in the break room — the question states no wireless in the break room. The phone must use cellular.
  • "Cable" alone is not a protocol — you must name the specific protocol (Ethernet, USB, HDMI).
  • Lines without labels are worth zero. Every. Single. Line. Must. Have. A. Word.
2(d): Mobile Tethering for Cloud Storage 2m

An accountant has a smartphone and laptop and is sitting in a park. She needs to store her meeting notes (on her laptop) in the cloud. Describe how she can use her devices to store the notes in the cloud.

Context

She is outside — no Wi-Fi available. Her laptop doesn't have mobile internet. Only her phone has internet (via cellular). The answer is tethering: use the phone's mobile data to give the laptop internet access.

Key Terms & Concepts

Tethering — Using a smartphone's mobile data to provide internet access to another device (laptop, tablet).
Mobile hotspot — A phone acting as a Wi-Fi access point — other devices connect to it via Wi-Fi.
Ad hoc wireless — A temporary wireless network created on-the-fly between two devices.
Personal Area Network (PAN) — A short-range network between a user's own devices — e.g. phone and laptop connected via Bluetooth tethering.

✓ Mark Scheme Answer

Award up to two marks for a linked description such as:

  • Tethering / creation of an ad hoc wireless access point / mobile hotspot / PAN (via Bluetooth) (1) AND use the phone's cellular / mobile / 3G/4G network to access the internet / the cloud (1)

Mark Breakdown: Tethering method (1) + cellular/mobile network (1). Both halves required.

💡 Hints & Exam Tips

  • Two halves: (a) how the phone shares with the laptop (hotspot/tethering), (b) what the phone uses to reach the internet (cellular/mobile data).
  • Accept "broadband" as a synonym for cellular in this context.
  • Write it as a single linked sentence: "She tethers her phone as a mobile hotspot, using its 4G/cellular connection to give the laptop internet access to reach the cloud."

⚠️ Common Mistakes

  • Saying "connect to Wi-Fi" alone doesn't work — there is no Wi-Fi in the park. You must explain that the phone creates the hotspot using its cellular data.
  • Saying "use ethernet" is wrong — ethernet is wired; no cables in the park.
  • Only one half of the linked description earns 1 mark. You need both to earn 2.

Question 3: Databases, SQL, Smart Cities & Software

Total Marks: 18 | Exam Time: ~24 minutes

📘 Context

Bookstores use networks and software applications. This question covers database keys, data types, SQL queries, smart cities, and a 6-mark essay on open-source vs proprietary software.

3(a)(i): Database Keys (Table) 4m

Complete the table to give the field names of the keys described. Tables: tbl_employee, tbl_store, tbl_shift.

Context

You're given three partial tables. You need to identify: (1) tbl_employee primary key; (2) tbl_store primary key; (3) tbl_shift foreign key; (4) tbl_shift composite key.

Key Terms & Concepts

Primary key — A field (or combination of fields) that uniquely identifies every row in a table. Usually an ID/number.
Foreign key — A field in one table that refers to the primary key of another table — used to link tables together.
Composite key — A primary key made up of multiple fields combined, because no single field is unique enough on its own.

Step-by-Step Walkthrough

  1. tbl_employee: Each row is one employee. The unique ID is empNum.
  2. tbl_store: Each row is one store. The unique ID is storeNum.
  3. tbl_shift foreign key: Look for fields that match primary keys of other tables. "store" matches tbl_store.storeNum. "employee" matches tbl_employee.empNum. Either is accepted (but not both together).
  4. tbl_shift composite key: You need to find the minimum set of fields that makes every row unique. Look at sample data:
    • Row 2: store=73, date=19-05-2023, start=11:00, employee=594998
    • Row 4: store=73, date=19-05-2023, start=11:00, employee=838485
    • Store+date+start alone is NOT unique (these two rows have the same values!). You MUST add employee to disambiguate.
  5. So composite key = employee, date, start. "end" is not needed (it's a consequence of the shift, not part of its identity).

✓ Mark Scheme Answer

  • tbl_employee Primary key: empNum (1 mark)
  • tbl_store Primary key: storeNum (1 mark)
  • tbl_shift Foreign key: store OR employee (1 mark) — either answer, but not both
  • tbl_shift Composite key: employee, date, start (1 mark) — all three required, order and punctuation ignored

Mark Breakdown: 1 mark per correct cell (4 cells total)

💡 Hints & Exam Tips

  • Primary keys are usually named things like "ID", "Num", "Number" — look for fields ending in those.
  • For composite keys, the test is: "what is the minimum combination that makes each row unique?" Try each combination against the sample data.
  • Foreign key row 3: either "store" or "employee" is correct — you don't need both (and writing both still earns only 1 mark).

⚠️ Common Mistakes

  • Do NOT include "end" in the composite key — it is explicitly disallowed by the mark scheme.
  • Don't write "both" for foreign key — pick one.
  • A composite key of just "store, date, start" is wrong — the sample data proves it's not unique.
3(a)(ii): Leading Zeros Data Type 2m

The empNum field must be 6 digits long. One possible value is 062833. Explain one reason the empNum field cannot be assigned a data type of integer.

Context

The example value 062833 is your clue — it has a leading zero. Integers cannot store leading zeros because 062833 and 62833 are numerically the same number (sixty-two thousand eight hundred and thirty-three). If stored as an integer, the "0" would be dropped and the value would become 62833 — only 5 digits, breaking the 6-digit rule.

Key Terms & Concepts

Integer — A whole-number data type. Cannot store leading zeros, decimal points, or characters other than digits.
String / text — A data type that stores any sequence of characters — numbers, letters, symbols. Preserves leading zeros.
Fixed-length field — A field that must always contain exactly N characters — typical for IDs, postcodes, account numbers.

✓ Mark Scheme Answer

Award up to two marks for a linked explanation such as:

  • The 0 at the start of the employee number would be dropped (1) because numbers / integers cannot have leading 0s / values with leading 0s must be stored as strings or characters (1)

Mark Breakdown: Effect (1) + reason (1). Both halves must associate.

💡 Hints & Exam Tips

  • Structure: [what happens] because [why it happens].
  • Use exact wording: "leading zero is dropped" / "integers cannot store leading zeros" / "must be stored as a string".
  • The question gives you 062833 as a worked example — refer to it directly: "The leading 0 in 062833 would be lost if stored as an integer".

⚠️ Common Mistakes

  • Do not accept examples alone — you must explain why. Just writing "062833" as an answer isn't enough.
  • Writing only the reason (e.g. "integers can't have leading zeros") without the effect (what actually happens to the data) loses the first mark.
  • Writing only the effect (e.g. "it wouldn't work properly") without the reason loses the second mark.
3(b)(i): SQL UNION Query 2m

Write an SQL statement that returns a set of unique titles available in one or both formats (books and DVDs).

Context

The database has two separate tables: tbl_book (with a title field) and tbl_dvd (also with a title field). You need to combine both lists into one set of titles with no duplicates — this is what UNION does.

Key Terms & Concepts

UNION — SQL keyword that combines the results of two SELECT queries into a single result set, removing duplicates.
UNION ALL — Combines but keeps duplicates. Not what you want here since the question says "unique".
SELECT ... FROM — Basic SQL syntax to retrieve fields from a table.

SQL Query Step-by-Step

  1. You cannot SELECT from two tables in one statement (unless joining, which is different). So you write TWO separate SELECT statements.
  2. First: SELECT title FROM tbl_book
  3. Then the keyword: UNION
  4. Then the second SELECT: SELECT title FROM tbl_dvd
  5. UNION automatically removes duplicates, so "The Plan" (in both tables) appears once.

✓ Mark Scheme Answer

Award 1 mark for each of:

Key Terms & Concepts

Flowchart symbols (Appendix 7) — Oval = start/stop; Rectangle = process; Parallelogram = input/output; Diamond = decision (exactly 1 input, 2 outputs labelled yes/no); Arrow = flow direction.

Fully connected — Every symbol must be reachable from Start and every path must reach an End — no hanging symbols or dead ends.

  1. Start (oval) → Display blank registration form (process rectangle)
  2. Accept first name, last name, email inputs (parallelogram I/O)
  3. Decision diamond: Cancel or Submit? → Cancel branch ends at End (oval). Submit branch continues.
  4. Validate form (process rectangle)
  5. Decision: Error on form? → Yes: Show error (parallelogram) → loop back to "accept inputs". No: continue.

  8. Generate unique registration number (process rectangle)
  9. Show registration number to customer (parallelogram I/O)
  10. End (oval)

  • D2: Cancel and submit both handled (any order) (1)

  • D6: Accurate use of flowchart notation (Appendix 7) throughout (1) — start/stop symbols, decision boxes with 1 input / 2 outputs with yes/no labels, fully connected with arrows/lines (no hanging symbols)
